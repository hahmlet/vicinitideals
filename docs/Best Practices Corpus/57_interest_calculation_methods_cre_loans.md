# 30/360, Actual/365, and Actual/360 - How Lenders Calculate Interest on CRE Loans
Source: https://www.adventuresincre.com/lenders-calcs/
Reading Time: 7 min

Commercial real estate lenders commonly calculate loans in three ways: 30/360, Actual/365 (aka 365/365), and Actual/360 (aka 365/360). Real estate professionals should be aware of these methods if they want to understand the real interest rate as well as the total amount of interest being paid over the term of a loan.

## Overview

If three different lenders are offering identical loans with identical loan amounts and interest rates, but each lender is using one of the three interest calculations, a borrower will pay the least interest with 30/360 and the most with Actual/360.

Example: $1,000,000 loan at 4% for 10 years with no IO period.

### 30/360

Take the annual interest rate (4%) and divide by 360 to get the daily interest rate (0.0111%). Then multiply by 30 to get the monthly interest rate (0.333%).

This method assumes 360 days per year and 30 days per month. Returns a true 4% interest rate. Simplest approach: just divide the annual rate by 12.

### Actual/365 (365/365)

Take the annual interest rate and divide by 365, then multiply by the actual number of days in the current month.

Example for February: 4% / 365 = 0.0110% daily, x 28 days = 0.307% monthly (0.318% on a leap year).

Although dividing by 365 gives a smaller daily rate, multiplying by actual days (sometimes 31) results in slightly more total interest than 30/360 due to leap years.

### Actual/360 (365/360)

The annual interest rate is divided by 360 (creating a larger daily rate) then multiplied by the actual days in the month. This creates a larger dollar amount in interest payments.

This method has seen legal challenges from borrowers who argued it was deceptive, but lenders prevailed because the methodology was fully disclosed.

Effective interest rates over a 4-year period: 365/365 = 4.003%, 365/360 = 4.058%.

## Key Takeaway

Always defer to the loan documentation and review the explicit methodology for how interest will be calculated. The terminologies used may mean slightly different things to different people, but the documentation will always be explicit.
