# Elon Musk's Automation Rules for CRE + The Multiplier Framework
Source: https://www.adventuresincre.com/automation-principles-multiplier-framework/
Reading Time: 13 min

AI is finally good enough that you can now leverage it to realistically multiply your output in CRE.

The AI Multiplier Framework is our method for doing just that. It gives you a structured way to find, rank, and execute automation opportunities so you can actually hit a meaningful multiplier in 12 months.

If the Multiplier Framework tells you what to automate and in what order, Elon Musk's five automation principles give you a proven playbook for how to redesign a specific workflow before you touch AI.

Used together, they keep you from making the classic mistake in process automation: Automating a bad process and locking the pain in forever.

## A Quick Recap: The Multiplier Framework

Treat your time like a financial asset.
- Step 1 - Audit: List your recurring deliverables and the tasks required for each one.
- Step 2 - Quantify: Put time and dollar values on those tasks.
- Step 3 - Identify: Find potential automation solutions.
- Step 4 - Prioritize: Rank tasks with an Impact Score so you know where to start.
- Step 5 - Execute: Move tasks through Manual -> Implementing -> Iterating -> Automating.

That framework sits at the portfolio level. It tells you where the value is. Musk's principles sit at the task level. It tells you how to design what you are about to build.

## Musk's 5 Principles of Automation, Adapted For CRE

1. Make the requirements less dumb
2. Delete the part or process
3. Simplify and optimize
4. Accelerate cycle time
5. Automate

The order matters. Most people start at step 5. That is why most automation projects stall or produce marginal gains.

### Step 1: Make The Requirements Less Dumb

Before you automate anything, question what the deliverable is supposed to do and who it is actually for.

Examples:
- Quarterly asset report: Do investors really read 25 pages, or do they care about 6 charts and 2 paragraphs?
- BOV package: Does the client need three different formats of the rent roll, or did someone ask for that once and it became "the way we do things"?

Questions to ask: If I were designing this today, with a blank slate, what would this deliverable look like? Who reads it, and which sections change their decisions?

### Step 2: Delete The Part Or Process

The bias in CRE is always toward more: more checks, more tabs, more approvals, more slides. Aggressively remove steps before you simplify or automate anything.

- IC Memos: Drop legacy appendices that are never discussed in committee. Remove duplicate model outputs.
- Lease abstraction: Stop abstracting fields that never show up in decisions for a given asset class.

### Step 3: Simplify And Optimize

Once you have questioned requirements and deleted junk, tighten what remains:
- Standardize templates: One IC memo template per strategy with fixed section order.
- Standardize inputs: Keep rent rolls, T-12s, and OM inputs in consistent formats.
- Batch and sequence work: Pull comps for all active BOVs once a week instead of one at a time.

You should be able to explain the workflow to a new analyst in a couple of minutes. If you cannot describe it simply, you are not ready for step 5.

### Step 4: Accelerate Cycle Time

Reduce the delay between data in and first draft out, first draft and review, review and final signoff.
- Move from ad hoc email reviews to scheduled review blocks.
- Use checklists for BOVs, ICs, and closing memos.
- Cut down the number of people who must sign off.

### Step 5: Automate

Only now should you plug in AI tools:
- General LLMs: ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini for drafts, summaries, and checks
- CRE-specific AI: CRE Agents tasks, AI.Edge prompts, data integrations
- Workflow tools: Zapier, Make, n8n to connect your CRM, email, project tools
- Document AI: For leases, OMs, lender term sheets, legal docs

You are no longer trying to "use AI somewhere." You are telling the AI exactly what input to expect, what output to produce, and where that output goes next.

## Example: IC Memo

Legacy IC Memo: 60-80 pages, 100+ man-hours, comprehensive data dump, slow decisions, manual copy-paste.
"Algorithm" IC Memo: 15-25 pages + targeted appendices, 20-30 man-hours, decision-driving variables, fast decisions, linked model outputs and AI-generated summaries.

The win is not "producing a 100-page memo faster." The win is realizing that a tight 20-page memo is a better decision tool, then automating the production of those 20 pages from your models and data sources.

## How To Start

1. Pick your top 3 automation targets from your prioritized list.
2. For each one, write out the current process in simple steps.
3. Run those steps through Musk's five questions.
4. Only then move the task to "Implementing."

Automation becomes the last 20 percent of the work, not the first 80.
