# From Risk-Free To IRR, From Rates to Spreads
Source: https://www.adventuresincre.com/from-risk-free-to-irr/
Reading Time: 14 min

When analyzing real estate investments, understanding key financial concepts and metrics like the risk-free rate, cap rate, discount rate (or IRR), yield-on-cost, and risk premium (i.e. spread) is essential for making informed decisions. These concepts not only quantify risks and returns but also reveal the nuanced trade-offs that drive investment success.

## What is the Risk-Free Rate and Why Does it Matter?

The risk-free rate serves as the foundational benchmark for evaluating investment returns. It represents the return an investor can expect to earn without assuming any risk of financial loss. Typically, U.S. Treasury bills (T-bills) or bonds are used as proxies for the risk-free rate.

For example, if the 10-year U.S. Treasury (UST) yield is 3.5%, this rate establishes the baseline return against which other investments are measured.

Why It Matters:
- Benchmark for Investment Evaluation: The risk-free rate is the benchmark against which all other investments are measured.
- Determining Hurdle Rates: Investors and firms use the risk-free rate to set minimum acceptable returns (hurdle rates).
- Impact on Discount Rates: In DCF analysis, the risk-free rate is a critical component in determining the discount rate.

## Understanding Real Estate Rates and Spreads: Cap Rate Basics

The capitalization rate (cap rate) is a fundamental valuation metric that indicates the untrended return on an investment property based on its Net Operating Income (NOI) relative to its purchase price.

Formula: Property Value = Stabilized NOI / Cap Rate

Example: If a property generates an NOI of $1,000,000 and is priced at $20,000,000, the cap rate is: $1,000,000 / $20,000,000 = 5.0%

Key Points:
- Risk and Illiquidity Premium: Unlike the risk-free rate, the cap rate incorporates the inherent risks and illiquidity associated with real estate investments.
- Market Comparisons: Cap rates vary by property type, location, and market conditions.
- Indicator of Market Trends: Declining cap rates may indicate increasing property values and investor demand; rising cap rates might suggest declining values or increased perceived risks.

## Discount Rate and IRR

The Internal Rate of Return (IRR) is a key financial metric used to evaluate the profitability of an investment over time. It represents the annualized rate of return expected from an investment, accounting for both the timing and magnitude of cash flows.

Key Concepts:
- Discount Rate vs. IRR: The discount rate is used to calculate the present value of future cash flows in DCF analysis. The IRR is the specific discount rate that makes NPV = 0.
- The Discount Rate is the target return (i.e. the target IRR) of the investment.

Example: A property with a cap rate of 5.0% and projected annual cash flow growth of 2%. If the IRR is 7.0%, this indicates a 2.0% higher return than the current cap rate, reflecting anticipated income growth and potential property appreciation.

Relationship with Cap Rate: While the cap rate provides a snapshot of a property's stabilized yield, the IRR incorporates future growth and exit strategies, offering a more dynamic assessment.

## Understanding Real Estate Rate Spreads

Key Spreads in Real Estate:

1. Cap Rate vs. Risk-Free Rate: The spread indicates the additional yield an investor earns for taking on the inherent risks of real estate compared to a risk-free asset. Example: Cap rate 5.0% - UST 3.5% = 1.5% (150 bps). This premium compensates for risks such as tenant turnover, lease defaults, and property management.

2. IRR vs. Cap Rate: The spread captures additional returns anticipated from income growth, operational efficiencies, and property appreciation. Example: IRR 7.0% - Cap rate 5.0% = 2.0% (200 bps).

3. Lender Spreads: Lenders determine mortgage rates by adding a spread to the risk-free rate. Example: Mortgage 5.5% - UST 3.5% = 2.0% (200 bps).

## Case Study: GreenShield Investments

GreenShield Investments is evaluating the acquisition of Midwest Fulfillment Center, a 200,000 SF warehouse in Chicago, IL. Offered at $20M with NOI of $1M (5.0% cap rate). The 10-year UST yield is 3.5%.

Cap Rate vs Risk-Free Rate: 5.0% - 3.5% = 1.5% spread. Reflects premium for illiquid, riskier real estate over Treasury bonds.

IRR vs Cap Rate: 7.0% - 5.0% = 2.0% spread. Represents additional return from income growth and property appreciation.

Overall Spread: IRR - Risk-Free = 7.0% - 3.5% = 3.5% (350 bps) total premium over risk-free rate.

The 1.5% spread over risk-free ensures a baseline premium for a stable industrial asset, while the additional 2.0% from IRR accounts for projected rent growth. GreenShield views a 7.0% unlevered IRR as appropriate for its core-plus risk profile.

## Final Notes

These financial metrics create a framework for understanding investment risks and returns:
- Risk-Free Rate: Establishes the baseline return with no risk.
- Cap Rate: Reflects fixed, stabilized yields.
- Discount Rate/IRR: Incorporates cash flow variability and growth.
- Spread: Quantifies the risk premium for illiquidity, uncertainty, or cash flow dynamics.
