# Understanding Stabilization Through The Lens Of Operating Expense Ratio
Source: https://www.adventuresincre.com/understanding-stabilization-through-lens-operating-expense-ratio/
Reading Time: 12 min

The term "stabilized" is deeply embedded in the language of real estate. While stabilization is commonly associated with occupancy thresholds or leasing milestones, its definition extends well beyond these surface metrics. True stabilization occurs at the intersection of operational efficiency, market convention, and financial durability.

## Stabilization: It's about More Than Just Occupancy

In most introductory discussions, stabilization is defined as the point at which a property reaches a certain level of consistent occupancy (e.g. 95% for multifamily, 90% for office). But from a financial standpoint, occupancy alone is insufficient. A fully leased property that is poorly managed, leased at below market rents, or subject to irregular cost burdens can still be cash-flow negative.

A stabilized asset isn't just "not broken." It's an asset that's consistently performing at its expected or market-appropriate level, generating enough NOI to support ongoing obligations and returns.

True stabilization must reflect both consistency and sufficiency.

## The Operating Expense Ratio Reality Check

The OER reflects how much of a property's revenue is consumed by operating costs. For many property types and markets, this figure ranges from 20% to 50%.

Formula: OER = Operating Expenses / Effective Gross Revenue

An OER of 86% means that for every $1.00 of effective gross income, $0.86 is spent just to keep the lights on before capital expenditures or debt service.

## The Role Of Market Comparables

Stabilization is a market-relative concept. An 86% OER might seem high in most U.S. office submarkets, but what if that's the norm in a particular locale with unusually high insurance premiums or a local tax regime that inflates OpEx?

Even so, an entire market operating at breakeven is not sustainable and will likely face eventual correction.

## Unlevered Metrics, Levered Realities

The OER is calculated before debt service. Even if a property is marginally cash flow positive on an unlevered basis, it may still be unsustainable once financing is layered in. True financial stabilization requires consistent net operating income and enough margin to absorb capex, debt, and provide return.

## Four Pillars of True Stabilization

1. Sustained Occupancy that Signals Market Demand - Not artificially boosted by temporary concessions. Requires durable tenancy and reliable, recurring income streams.

2. Operational Efficiency Benchmarked to Market Standards - An office building with a 50% OER in a market where the norm is 35% raises concerns, even if fully leased. Must ask: "Is this asset performing in line with what the market considers normal?"

3. Strong NOI to Support Reinvestment and Financial Commitments - Must generate enough NOI to cover debt service and fund necessary capital expenditures. Should not be a razor-thin margin. Lenders and equity investors expect a buffer (DSCR).

4. Distributable Levered Cash Flow that Sustains Returns - The ultimate test: does the property produce positive cash flow after debt service and all capital obligations?

If any of these pillars are missing, the property may be leased and functioning, but it is not financially stabilized. These assets occupy the gray space between "core" and "value-add" (i.e. core-plus).

## Case Study: Sierra Ridge Office Park

At acquisition: 88% occupied, $2.25M EGI, $1.92M operating expenses, OER = 85.3%, NOI = $330K. Barely treading water.

After $3.2M renovation + operational improvements: 93% occupied, $2.62M EGI, $1.36M operating expenses, OER = 51.9%, NOI = $1.26M (nearly 4x increase).

Only after the turnaround could the property be considered truly stabilized.

## Key Takeaway

A high OER is a diagnostic clue. In the right analytical hands, it's a signal that helps us think more critically about the assets we underwrite, manage, and invest in. To ask whether a property is stabilized is not to inquire about its current occupancy alone, but to ask whether it can fulfill its obligations, support its capital stack, and generate acceptable returns over time.
